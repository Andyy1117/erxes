It‚Äôs not groundbreaking news that technologies developed over the past quarter-century have dramatically reshaped how businesses operate and how they interact with customers. While these vehicles have undoubtedly helped drive centuries of growth and innovation, they tend to serve the interests of a privileged few ‚Äî wealthy shareholders over stakeholders.

Companies wanted to reward or incentivize their stakeholders by offering a stake in their future success. But there currently exists no vehicle for them to do so. Pre-IPO companies frequently sell shares to their employees as a retention perk, but securities regulations prevent them from offering shares to many outside stakeholders. There are about half as many public companies today (3,671) as in 1996 (7,322). If a company doesn‚Äôt go public, sell itself, or distribute dividends, its financial value remains perpetually in the hands of a few. Stakeholders never get their fair share.
